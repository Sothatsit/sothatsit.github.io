My Love of Constraints

I have always been fascinated with constraints. When I was little, I often tested the constraints of my parents, and now, I love using constraints to type-check my programs, make logical inferences from data, and to find bugs in my programs before they raise hard-to-debug errors. Constraints bring order to complex systems.

However, with the recent advancements in AI coding assistants such as GitHub CoPilot and ChatGPT (amazing btw!), it seems that we have been largely ignoring the value of constraints. I have even seen the sentiment that we just have to wait for the AI to get better, and then the AI will solve all the constraints itself. Frankly, I think this is very optimistic.

However, I understand why people think this.

These assistants do such a great job of predicting short snippets of code, and they are getting better so fast, that it has seemed inevitable that they will take over everything. Also, constraints aren't nearly as trendy. Although, while the general utility of these coding assistants is unmatched by other techniques such as genetic programming, their reliability is not even close to 100%. In fact, their use has already been shown to lead to security vulnerabilities.

In this article, I hope to argue that there is a huge opportunity to leverage "old" logical constraint systems alongside the new modern coding assistants to build large, sophisticated systems more effectively. I also argue why a custom IDE would be needed, instead of just building this into a tool or plugin. Let's dive in!
